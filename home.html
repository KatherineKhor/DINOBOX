HISTORY
OF R&B

R&B can trace its roots back to the 1940s, when African American musicians began to blend blues and jazz with gospel music. The genre gained popularity in the 1950s, thanks to the success of artists such as Ray Charles, Sam Cooke, and James Brown. In the 1960s, Motown Records, founded by Berry Gordy, Jr., became a major force in R&B, with acts such as The Temptations, The Supremes, and Marvin Gaye achieving commercial success. In the 1980s and 1990s, R&B experienced a resurgence, with the emergence of artists such as Whitney Houston, Mariah Carey, and Boyz II Men. In the 2000s, R&B continued to evolve, with the rise of artists such as Beyoncé, Rihanna, and Usher.

/02
CHARACTERISTICS
OF R&B

R&B is characterized by its strong emphasis on rhythm and melody, as well as its soulful vocals. The genre typically features a steady beat, with prominent bass and drum lines. R&B songs often feature lyrics about love, relationships, and heartbreak. The use of electronic instruments, such as synthesizers and drum machines, has become more common in modern R&B.
